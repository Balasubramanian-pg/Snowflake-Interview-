# Providers vs. Consumers: The Core of Snowflake Data Sharing

In the world of Snowflake data sharing, every data sharing relationship involves two main roles: a **Data Provider** and a **Data Consumer**. This distinction is crucial because it defines who owns the data, who controls access, and who ultimately benefits from the shared information.

Think of it like this: The Provider is the one with the valuable information (like a library) and the Consumer is the one who wants to read or use that information (like a library patron).

### The Data Provider

The **Data Provider** is the Snowflake account that owns the data and initiates the sharing process. They are the source of truth for the shared datasets.

**Key Characteristics and Responsibilities of a Provider:**

1.  **Data Ownership:** The provider retains full ownership and control over the original data. The data never leaves the provider's account.
2.  **Data Curation:** Providers are responsible for organizing, cleaning, and structuring the data they intend to share. This might involve creating databases, schemas, tables, views, and especially secure views to present the data in a consumable format.
3.  **Share Creation:** They create `SHARE` objects, which are the fundamental units of data sharing in Snowflake. A share defines exactly which database objects (tables, secure views, user-defined functions) will be made available.
4.  **Access Management:** Providers grant access to their shares to specific consumer accounts. They control who can see what.
5.  **Compute Cost (for some scenarios):** If sharing with a Reader Account, the provider pays for the compute resources used by the consumer's queries. If sharing with another full Snowflake account, the consumer pays for their own compute.
6.  **Data Freshness:** Any updates or changes made by the provider to the original data are immediately reflected and accessible to consumers, ensuring real-time data access.
7.  **Monitoring & Auditing:** Providers can monitor usage of their shares and audit queries run by consumers.

**Provider's Perspective: Why Share Data?**

Providers share data for various strategic reasons:

*   **Monetization:** Selling access to proprietary datasets (e.g., market research data, weather data).
*   **Collaboration:** Working with partners, suppliers, or customers on joint projects.
*   **Internal Distribution:** Making data available across different departments or business units within a larger organization.
*   **Improved Products/Services:** Enhancing their own offerings by providing data directly to their users.
*   **Regulatory Compliance:** Providing specific data to auditing bodies or regulators.

**Example Provider Queries:**

Let's imagine a company, "Global Retail Co.," wants to share anonymized sales data with a market analysis firm.

1.  **Create a database and load/transform data (if not already done):**
    ```sql
    -- As Global Retail Co. (Provider Account)
    CREATE DATABASE GLOBAL_RETAIL_DB;
    USE GLOBAL_RETAIL_DB;
    CREATE SCHEMA SALES;
    CREATE TABLE SALES.DAILY_SALES (
        SALE_DATE DATE,
        PRODUCT_CATEGORY VARCHAR,
        REGION VARCHAR,
        TOTAL_SALES_USD DECIMAL(18, 2),
        UNITS_SOLD NUMBER
    );
    -- Assume data is loaded here via COPY INTO or ETL process
    ```

2.  **Create a Secure View to share specific data, potentially masking sensitive info or aggregating:**
    ```sql
    -- Provider wants to share aggregated daily sales by category and region,
    -- without exposing individual transaction details.
    CREATE SECURE VIEW SALES.AGGREGATED_DAILY_SALES AS
    SELECT
        SALE_DATE,
        PRODUCT_CATEGORY,
        REGION,
        SUM(TOTAL_SALES_USD) AS AGG_TOTAL_SALES_USD,
        SUM(UNITS_SOLD) AS AGG_UNITS_SOLD
    FROM SALES.DAILY_SALES
    GROUP BY SALE_DATE, PRODUCT_CATEGORY, REGION;
    ```

3.  **Create a Share object:**
    ```sql
    CREATE SHARE MARKET_ANALYST_SALES_SHARE
    COMMENT = 'Anonymized daily sales data for market analysis firm.';
    ```

4.  **Grant privileges to the Share:**
    ```sql
    -- Grant USAGE on the database that contains the objects to be shared
    GRANT USAGE ON DATABASE GLOBAL_RETAIL_DB TO SHARE MARKET_ANALYST_SALES_SHARE;

    -- Grant USAGE on the schema within that database
    GRANT USAGE ON SCHEMA GLOBAL_RETAIL_DB.SALES TO SHARE MARKET_ANALYST_SALES_SHARE;

    -- Grant SELECT on the specific secure view
    GRANT SELECT ON VIEW GLOBAL_RETAIL_DB.SALES.AGGREGATED_DAILY_SALES TO SHARE MARKET_ANALYST_SALES_SHARE;

    -- Note: If you were sharing a table directly, you'd GRANT SELECT ON TABLE ...
    ```

5.  **Add Consumer Account(s) to the Share:**
    Let's say the market analysis firm's Snowflake account locator is `XYZ12345`.
    ```sql
    ALTER SHARE MARKET_ANALYST_SALES_SHARE ADD ACCOUNTS = XYZ12345;

    -- If sharing with a Reader Account (managed by the Provider):
    -- First, create the managed account:
    -- CREATE MANAGED ACCOUNT MARKET_ANALYST_READER
    --   ADMIN_NAME = market_admin
    --   ADMIN_PASSWORD = 'password'
    --   COMMENT = 'Reader account for market analysis firm';
    -- Then, add it to the share:
    -- ALTER SHARE MARKET_ANALYST_SALES_SHARE ADD ACCOUNT = MARKET_ANALYST_READER;
    ```

### The Data Consumer

The **Data Consumer** is the Snowflake account that receives and queries the shared data. They leverage the data provided by others for their own analysis, reporting, or application development.

**Key Characteristics and Responsibilities of a Consumer:**

1.  **Data Access:** Consumers access data from an `IMPORTED DATABASE` created from the share. This database is read-only, meaning they cannot modify the original data.
2.  **Compute Cost:** If they are a full Snowflake account, they use their own virtual warehouses for querying the shared data, and thus pay for their own compute costs. If they are a Reader Account, the provider pays for the compute.
3.  **No Data Management:** Consumers generally don't need to worry about the underlying storage, performance tuning, or data updates of the shared data. That's the provider's responsibility.
4.  **Integration:** Consumers can integrate the shared data with their own internal datasets, create new views, or build applications on top of it.
5.  **Secure Queries:** All queries run by consumers respect the security and access controls set by the provider.

**Consumer's Perspective: Why Consume Data?**

Consumers consume data to gain insights, enhance their own data, or power their applications:

*   **Enrichment:** Combining external market data with internal sales data for deeper analysis.
*   **Operational Efficiency:** Accessing partner inventory data for just-in-time logistics.
*   **New Product Development:** Using public datasets to train machine learning models.
*   **Cost Savings:** Avoiding the need to collect, store, and maintain large datasets themselves.
*   **Real-time Insights:** Getting immediate updates on critical business metrics without batch delays.

**Example Consumer Queries:**

Now, let's look at the market analysis firm (Consumer Account `XYZ12345`) accessing the data.

1.  **Show available shares:**
    ```sql
    -- As Market Analysis Firm (Consumer Account)
    SHOW SHARES;
    -- This would show something like:
    -- | created_on | kind  | name                       | owner     | comment                            | to_accounts |
    -- |------------|-------|----------------------------|-----------|------------------------------------|-------------|
    -- | ...        | INBOUND | MARKET_ANALYST_SALES_SHARE | GLOBAL_RETAIL_CO | Anonymized daily sales data... |             |
    ```

2.  **Create a database from the inbound share:**
    ```sql
    CREATE DATABASE RETAIL_SALES_FROM_GLOBAL_RETAIL
    FROM SHARE GLOBAL_RETAIL_CO.MARKET_ANALYST_SALES_SHARE;
    ```
    *   Note: `GLOBAL_RETAIL_CO` here is the account name of the provider. The fully qualified share name is `provider_account_name.share_name`.

3.  **Query the shared data:**
    ```sql
    USE DATABASE RETAIL_SALES_FROM_GLOBAL_RETAIL;
    USE SCHEMA GLOBAL_RETAIL_DB.SALES; -- The schema structure is preserved from the provider

    SELECT
        SALE_DATE,
        PRODUCT_CATEGORY,
        SUM(AGG_TOTAL_SALES_USD) AS DAILY_CATEGORY_SALES
    FROM AGGREGATED_DAILY_SALES
    WHERE REGION = 'North America'
    AND SALE_DATE BETWEEN '2026-01-01' AND '2026-01-31'
    GROUP BY SALE_DATE, PRODUCT_CATEGORY
    ORDER BY SALE_DATE, PRODUCT_CATEGORY;
    ```

4.  **Join shared data with their own internal data (optional):**
    Let's say the consumer has their own table of `MARKET_TRENDS`.
    ```sql
    -- Assuming the consumer has a database named MY_ANALYTICS_DB
    USE DATABASE MY_ANALYTICS_DB;
    CREATE TABLE MARKET_TRENDS (
        TREND_DATE DATE,
        PRODUCT_CATEGORY VARCHAR,
        MARKET_INDEX DECIMAL(10, 2)
    );
    -- Data loaded into MARKET_TRENDS

    -- Now, join shared data with internal data
    SELECT
        s.SALE_DATE,
        s.PRODUCT_CATEGORY,
        s.AGG_TOTAL_SALES_USD,
        t.MARKET_INDEX
    FROM RETAIL_SALES_FROM_GLOBAL_RETAIL.GLOBAL_RETAIL_DB.SALES.AGGREGATED_DAILY_SALES s
    JOIN MY_ANALYTICS_DB.PUBLIC.MARKET_TRENDS t
        ON s.SALE_DATE = t.TREND_DATE AND s.PRODUCT_CATEGORY = t.PRODUCT_CATEGORY
    WHERE s.REGION = 'Europe'
    ORDER BY s.SALE_DATE, s.PRODUCT_CATEGORY;
    ```

### The Synergy: A Powerful Relationship

The Provider-Consumer model is powerful because it establishes a clear separation of concerns while enabling seamless collaboration:

*   **Decoupled Environments:** Both provider and consumer operate in their own Snowflake accounts, maintaining their own security, governance, and cost structures.
*   **Live Data:** The consumer always sees the most up-to-date version of the data without any replication or ETL overhead.
*   **Centralized Control:** The provider maintains complete control over their data, defining what is shared and with whom.
*   **Scalability:** Snowflake handles the underlying infrastructure, allowing both providers and consumers to scale their operations independently.

This relationship is the backbone of Snowflake's Data Cloud strategy, fostering an ecosystem where data can be easily discovered, accessed, and leveraged across organizational boundaries, driving innovation and efficiency. Whether it's through direct shares, Reader Accounts, or the Snowflake Marketplace, the Provider-Consumer dynamic remains the fundamental principle.
