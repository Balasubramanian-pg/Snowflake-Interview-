# What are Reader Accounts?

At its core, a Reader Account is a Snowflake account that a data provider (you, in this scenario) can create and manage for a data consumer. The key differentiator is that these consumers don't need to sign up for or pay for their own Snowflake account. Instead, they access your shared data through this Reader Account, which is essentially a stripped-down version of a standard Snowflake account.

It's a one-way sharing model: data flows from your provider account to the Reader Account. The consumer can query the shared data but cannot modify it or upload their own data into the Reader Account. They also can't create their own databases, warehouses, or other objects; they simply get access to what you've shared.

### Why are they so useful?

1.  **Simplified Access for Consumers:** This is probably the biggest win. Imagine you have a business partner, a vendor, or even an internal team that needs access to a specific dataset for reporting or analysis, but they don't have (or need) a full Snowflake account. Instead of cumbersome data extracts, FTP, or complex API integrations, you just give them credentials to a Reader Account. They log in, query the data, and they're good to go. No contracts, no setup costs for them, no administrative overhead for managing another full account.

2.  **Cost Efficiency:** For the data consumer, it's free. They incur no direct Snowflake charges. For the data provider, you control the virtual warehouse used by the Reader Account. This means you pay for the compute resources consumed by their queries, but you have full visibility and control over that cost. You can size the warehouse appropriately for their expected workload.

3.  **Enhanced Security and Governance:** You control exactly what data is shared and with whom. The shared data is secured by Snowflake's robust access controls. You share specific databases or schemas, not your entire account. This means you can confidently share data without worrying about exposing sensitive information they shouldn't see. Plus, all queries run by the Reader Account are logged in your provider account, giving you auditing capabilities.

4.  **Faster Time to Insight:** By removing the barriers of data transfer and separate account setup, consumers can get access to the data much faster, leading to quicker insights and more agile decision-making.

5.  **Seamless Experience:** The consumer experience is almost identical to a regular Snowflake user. They use the standard Snowflake UI, ODBC/JDBC drivers, or connectors to query the shared data. This familiarity reduces the learning curve.

### Architecture: How it all works

Let's break down the underlying mechanics.

1.  **Provider Account:** This is your primary Snowflake account, where your data resides. You own the data and manage the shares.
2.  **Share Object:** In your provider account, you create a `SHARE` object. This object specifies which databases, schemas, tables, views, and secure views you want to share. Crucially, you grant `USAGE` on the share to specific Reader Accounts.
3.  **Reader Account Creation:** You use the `CREATE MANAGED ACCOUNT` command in your provider account to provision a new Reader Account. When you create it, you assign it a unique name and specify a region.
4.  **Database from Share:** Once the Reader Account is created and granted access to a `SHARE`, a special read-only database is automatically created within the Reader Account. This database is a direct representation of the shared objects from the provider account.
5.  **Virtual Warehouse:** Each Reader Account requires a virtual warehouse for query processing. This warehouse is actually provisioned and managed by the provider account. The provider pays for the compute credits consumed by the Reader Account's queries.
6.  **Users in Reader Account:** You, as the provider, can create users within the Reader Account and assign them roles, just like in a regular Snowflake account. This allows you to manage who within the consumer organization can access the shared data.

#### Key Architectural Components:

*   **Managed Account:** The term Snowflake uses for a Reader Account, emphasizing that it's managed by the data provider.
*   **Share:** The mechanism for encapsulating and making data available.
*   **Virtual Warehouse:** The compute engine. In a Reader Account setup, the provider's account is billed for the warehouse used by the reader.
*   **Provider-Managed Users/Roles:** The provider can create and manage user credentials and roles within the Reader Account, offering fine-grained access control to the shared data.

### Creating and Managing Reader Accounts

The process generally involves these steps:

1.  **Prepare the Data (Provider Side):**
    *   Ensure the data you want to share is in a database within your provider account.
    *   Create secure views if you need to restrict column or row access to certain data.
    *   Grant necessary `SELECT` privileges on the tables/views to the role that will own the share.

2.  **Create a Share (Provider Side):**
    *   Use the `CREATE SHARE` command.
    *   Add the necessary objects (databases, schemas, tables, views) to the share using `GRANT ... TO SHARE`. Remember to grant `USAGE` on the database and schema, and `SELECT` on tables/views.

    ```sql
    CREATE SHARE my_data_share;
    GRANT USAGE ON DATABASE my_shared_db TO SHARE my_data_share;
    GRANT USAGE ON SCHEMA my_shared_db.public TO SHARE my_data_share;
    GRANT SELECT ON TABLE my_shared_db.public.sales_data TO SHARE my_data_share;
    ```

3.  **Create the Reader Account (Provider Side):**
    *   Use the `CREATE MANAGED ACCOUNT` command. You'll specify an account name, a user for the account (who will be the administrator for that Reader Account), and a password. You also specify the region.

    ```sql
    CREATE MANAGED ACCOUNT my_reader_account
      ADMIN_NAME = reader_admin
      ADMIN_PASSWORD = 'password123'
      COMMENT = 'Account for external partner X'
      REGION = 'aws_us_east_1'; -- Or your desired region
    ```

4.  **Grant Share Access to Reader Account (Provider Side):**
    *   Once the Reader Account is created, you grant the share to it.

    ```sql
    GRANT IMPORTED PRIVILEGES ON DATABASE my_shared_db TO ACCOUNT my_reader_account; -- No, this is wrong.
    -- The correct way is to grant the SHARE to the reader account.
    ALTER SHARE my_data_share ADD ACCOUNT = my_reader_account;
    ```
    *(Self-correction: The `GRANT IMPORTED PRIVILEGES` is for sharing within regular accounts. For Reader Accounts, you add the account to the share.)*

5.  **Provide Credentials to Consumer:**
    *   You give the consumer the account locator for the Reader Account, the `reader_admin` username, and the password you set.

6.  **Access Data (Consumer Side):**
    *   The consumer logs into the Reader Account using the provided credentials.
    *   They will see an `IMPORTED DATABASE` which maps to your shared data.
    *   They can then query the data using SQL.

    ```sql
    USE DATABASE MY_SHARED_DB; -- The imported database name often matches the original shared database name
    SELECT * FROM public.sales_data WHERE sale_date >= '2026-01-01';
    ```

### Managing Reader Accounts and Costs

As the data provider, you have several responsibilities and controls:

*   **Virtual Warehouse Management:**
    *   When a Reader Account is created, a default virtual warehouse is also created for it. You can manage this warehouse (e.g., resize it, suspend it) from your provider account.
    *   All compute costs for queries run in the Reader Account's warehouse are billed to your provider account. This is a critical point: the consumer doesn't pay for compute.
    *   You can monitor usage and costs through your account's usage dashboards and billing reports. This allows you to understand the cost implications of each Reader Account.

*   **User and Role Management:**
    *   You can log into the Reader Account as `reader_admin` (or any other administrative role you create for it) and create additional users and roles for the consumer's organization.
    *   This enables the consumer to manage their internal access to the shared data without you having to do it all, while you still maintain ultimate control.

*   **Data Updates:**
    *   When you update the data in your provider account that is part of a share, those updates are immediately available to the Reader Account users. There's no data replication or synchronization delay.

*   **Auditing and Monitoring:**
    *   All queries executed by users in the Reader Account are logged in your provider account's query history. This provides a full audit trail for compliance and monitoring.

*   **Deleting Reader Accounts:**
    *   You can easily drop a Reader Account using `DROP MANAGED ACCOUNT`. This immediately revokes access for all users of that account.

### Important Considerations and Best Practices

*   **Data Security:** While Reader Accounts simplify sharing, always adhere to the principle of least privilege. Share only the data necessary for the consumer's specific use case. Use secure views to filter rows and columns for even finer-grained control.
*   **Cost Monitoring:** Since you bear the compute costs, closely monitor the virtual warehouse usage of your Reader Accounts. If a consumer's query patterns are unexpectedly high, you might need to adjust the warehouse size or discuss usage with them.
*   **Documentation:** Provide clear documentation to your consumers on how to access the Reader Account, the schema of the shared data, and any specific usage guidelines.
*   **Communication:** Maintain open communication with your data consumers. Inform them of any schema changes or maintenance windows that might affect their access.
*   **Performance:** The performance experienced by the consumer depends on the size and configuration of the virtual warehouse you allocate to their Reader Account. Ensure it's adequately sized for their anticipated workload.
*   **No Data Ingress/Egress for Readers:** Remember, readers cannot load their own data into the Reader Account, nor can they unload data from it in an automated fashion (e.g., using `COPY INTO @stage`). They can, of course, copy query results from the UI or client tools, but not in bulk. This limitation is intentional for security and cost control.
*   **Geographic Considerations:** Reader Accounts must be in the same cloud provider (AWS, Azure, GCP) and region as the provider account. If you need to share across regions or cloud providers, you'd use standard direct shares between full Snowflake accounts, or look into Snowflake's Data Exchange.
*   **Reader Account Limitations:** Reader Accounts have a few limitations compared to full Snowflake accounts:
    *   No data loading (e.g., `COPY INTO`)
    *   No data unloading to stages
    *   Cannot create new databases, schemas, or tables (only the provider can add objects to the shared database)
    *   Cannot install client drivers or connectors directly into the Reader Account; they use their local setup.
    *   Cannot purchase data from the Snowflake Marketplace. They only consume what you share.

### Use Cases for Reader Accounts

Reader Accounts shine in scenarios where you need to share specific, often read-only, datasets with a limited number of external or internal parties without burdening them with Snowflake account setup or billing.

*   **Business Partners/Vendors:** Sharing sales data with a marketing agency, product usage data with a development partner, or supply chain metrics with a logistics provider.
*   **Customer Reporting:** Providing customized reports or dashboards to key customers based on their usage or transactional data.
*   **Internal Departments:** Offering departmental access to specific datasets without requiring each department to manage its own Snowflake environment.
*   **Auditing/Compliance:** Providing read-only access to auditors for specific compliance checks.
*   **Proof-of-Concept (POC) Sharing:** Quickly sharing data for a limited-time POC with a potential partner without extensive setup.
*   **Data Demonstrations:** Allowing prospective clients to interact directly with sample datasets without granting full access.

### Comparison to Other Sharing Methods

It's helpful to understand where Reader Accounts fit in compared to other Snowflake data sharing mechanisms:

*   **Direct Share (Account-to-Account):** This is for sharing data between two full Snowflake accounts. Both accounts pay for their own compute. It offers more capabilities (e.g., consumers can also re-share data, load/unload data if permissions allow). Use this when the consumer is already a Snowflake customer or needs more advanced capabilities.
*   **Snowflake Data Marketplace:** A public or private marketplace for data providers to list and monetize data products. Consumers subscribe to data feeds. This is for broader distribution and commercialization of data.
*   **Data Exchange:** A private, curated data sharing hub for a specific group of organizations. It acts like a mini-marketplace for a closed ecosystem.

Reader Accounts are distinct because they specifically cater to scenarios where the consumer *does not have* or *does not need* a full Snowflake account and the provider wants to manage all costs and infrastructure.

### The Evolution of Data Sharing

Reader Accounts represent a significant leap in how organizations can share data. Historically, data sharing was a painful process involving:

*   **FTP/SFTP:** Manual, insecure, batch-oriented, and often led to data staleness.
*   **APIs:** Required development effort, often complex to manage, and still involved moving data.
*   **ETL/ELT:** Extracting, transforming, and loading data into another system, which is resource-intensive and creates data silos.
*   **Emailing Spreadsheets:** A security and governance nightmare.

Snowflake's data sharing capabilities, including Reader Accounts, change this paradigm by enabling "live" data sharing. The data never leaves your account; consumers are simply given a window into it. This live access ensures data freshness, consistency, and a single source of truth, dramatically improving collaboration and decision-making speed.

### Conclusion

Reader Accounts are a powerful and flexible feature within Snowflake's data sharing ecosystem. They empower data providers to securely and efficiently share specific datasets with non-Snowflake customers or internal teams, reducing friction, accelerating time to insight, and maintaining tight control over security and costs. If you need to enable read-only data access for partners who don't need their own Snowflake account, Reader Accounts are an incredibly compelling solution. They simplify the entire process, making data collaboration truly seamless.
